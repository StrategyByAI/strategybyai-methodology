# What's Wrong with AI Strategy Today

## Three Structural Failures in Current Practice

The AI strategy market in 2025–2026 is dominated by tools that automate existing strategic processes rather than creating new strategic capabilities. This document diagnoses three structural failures and explains how Strategy by AI addresses each one.

---

## Failure 1: Automation of Existing Processes, Not New Strategic Patterns

### The current state

AI strategy platforms (Quantive, Cascade, Perdoo, and similar) digitise the strategic planning workflow that existed before AI: goal setting, OKR alignment, performance dashboards, progress tracking. They make existing processes faster and more visible.

What they do not do is generate new strategic patterns that exploit AI's distinctive capabilities:

- **Accumulated knowledge across thousands of historical cases.** LLMs are trained on strategic literature spanning Sun Tzu through Clausewitz through modern competitive analysis. This knowledge exists in the model but is never systematically activated by current tools.
- **Scope of simultaneous attention.** An LLM can cross-reference hundreds of pages of evidence, dozens of competitive entities, and multiple temporal horizons in a single analytical pass. Current tools use AI for single-variable monitoring, not multi-variable strategic reasoning.
- **Pattern recognition across domains.** Military strategy, political campaigns, and business competition share structural patterns (escalation dynamics, culmination timing, centre of gravity formation) that LLMs can recognise — but only when directed to look.

### How Strategy by AI addresses this

The methodology provides structured reasoning frameworks that activate LLM pre-trained strategic knowledge for outputs no current tool produces:

- **Enemy classification across four threat levels** (alien → rival → adversary → enemy) based on confrontation character — not generic "competitor analysis" treating all rivals equally
- **Capacity mathematics** calculating whether an organisation can physically respond to its threat environment — revealing structural impossibilities invisible to conventional analysis
- **Culmination timing prediction** forecasting when organisational capability peaks relative to decisive engagement — determining whether the organisation has enough time to win
- **Centre of gravity identification** locating where the war will be decided — the specific competitive point whose capture or loss determines the outcome

These are strategic patterns derived from military science that current AI tools neither attempt nor produce.

## Failure 2: Advisory Character — Analysis Without Governance

### The current state

Current AI strategy tools operate in advisory mode: they analyse data, identify trends, generate recommendations, and present dashboards. The human team then decides what to do, how to do it, and whether to persist or change course.

This creates a structural gap between AI insight and organisational action. The AI knows the situation has changed; the organisation hasn't responded. The AI recommends resource reallocation; no one has authority to act on it. The AI detects strategy drift; there's no governance mechanism to correct it.

The advisory model works for tools that inform human decisions. It fails for strategic execution, which requires continuous governance — the systematic, authoritative direction of organisational behaviour toward strategic objectives under pressure.

### How Strategy by AI addresses this

Module Six (Execution of the Strategy) is not advisory. It is a governance framework with three structural features current tools lack:

**Tier-structured decision authority:**
- Tier 1 (War-level): Strategy change, settlement decisions, fundamental transformation — human authority only, AI provides analysis
- Tier 2 (Operational): Campaign resource reallocation, objective priority adjustment — shared authority, AI proposes, human approves
- Tier 3 (Tactical): Campaign-internal adjustments within allocated parameters — AI-guided with human oversight

**Document activation protocols:** Each execution challenge (command crisis, friction, culmination approach, fog of war) triggers prescribed activation of specific documents from the complete portfolio, ensuring decisions reference documented evidence rather than improvised judgement.

**Continuous execution assessment:** Nine dimensions of execution are monitored and graded — commitment, command, cohesion, tempo/friction, centre of gravity, culmination, fog/chaos, adaptation, and termination. Each dimension has explicit adequacy criteria, producing a systematic performance assessment rather than subjective evaluation.

The AI assistant in Module Six does not recommend actions and wait. It activates documents, identifies decision requirements, classifies decisions by tier, proposes responses with evidence citations, monitors implementation, and flags when execution diverges from strategic design.

## Failure 3: Strategy Formulation Weakness

### The current state

Current AI strategy tools are strongest at the endpoints of the strategic process:

- **Data gathering** (competitive monitoring, market intelligence, news tracking)
- **Performance measurement** (KPI dashboards, execution tracking, goal progress)

They are weakest in the intellectual middle — the work of *formulating strategy*:

- Defining what war the organisation is fighting (and distinguishing it from the wider confrontation)
- Setting strategic objectives with specific mode, subject, and foe designations
- Designing campaign architectures with resource allocations, dependencies, and timelines
- Selecting targets based on accessibility, criticality, and offence-defence assessment
- Specifying form and manner of strategic action (offensive/defensive, direct/indirect)
- Integrating strategic principles (dislocation, asymmetry, initiative, manoeuvre, economy of forces)

These tasks require structured reasoning frameworks that current tools do not provide. Generic LLM prompting produces plausible-sounding but methodologically inconsistent strategy recommendations. Specialised platforms track execution of strategies they assume already exist.

### How Strategy by AI addresses this

Module Five (Making the Strategy) is a complete formulation engine producing four documents that constitute an executable strategic design:

**Document 21 — War Definition:** Segregates the wider confrontation from the bounded war. Classifies conflict type. Identifies war phase. Maps configuration across factor clustering, actor distribution, and trend vectors. Prevents mission creep by establishing explicit boundaries.

**Document 22 — Strategic Objectives Hierarchy:** Formulates objectives with three required elements (mode of action, subject of transformation, foe to overcome). Establishes hierarchy from supreme objective through primary, secondary, and tertiary objectives. Identifies and prioritises targets. Formally excludes unassailable targets to prevent resource waste.

**Document 23 — Campaign Architecture:** Segments strategy into coordinated campaigns. Allocates resources with specific percentages and budgets. Specifies timelines with dependencies. Determines form (offensive/defensive) and manner (direct/indirect) for each campaign.

**Document 24 — Strategy Integration:** Synthesises Documents 21–23 with strategic principles application. Matches strategy types to war phases. Structures 60-month timeframe. Specifies settlement conditions with honest probability assessment (including defeat probability).

The formulation output is not recommendations. It is a complete operational blueprint with specific parameters, timelines, resources, and measurable objectives — every element traceable to analytical evidence from Modules Two through Four.
